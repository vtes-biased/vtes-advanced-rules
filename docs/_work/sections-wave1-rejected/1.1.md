### 1.1 Card text vs. rules

**Base rules.** Whenever the cards contradict the rules, the cards take precedence
[RBK important-terms-in-the-game]. Precedence is triggered by contradiction, not by
silence: a card overrides the rulebook only on the point its text actually addresses,
and grants only what it says. Everything the card does not speak to continues to run on
the base rules. The Golden Rule settles card against rulebook; it says nothing about one
card against another.

#### 1.1.1 Mandatory and optional effects

The test is grammatical, and it is the whole test. Card text in the indicative states an
effect that happens; only a permissive verb ("may", "can", "you choose", "optional")
makes it a choice. A player never gains a right to decline an effect from the fact that
declining would suit him. This is the single most-repeated ruling in the corpus — it
lands identically on triggered card effects, on a granted press and on a granted
additional strike.[^1-1-1]

Three consequences follow, and they are where the rule is actually contested:

- **Mandatory holds against the controller's own interest, without limit.** A card that
  obliges you to pay a cost obliges you to pay it out of a pool you cannot afford; if
  paying ousts you, you are ousted. There is no implied "if able" and no implied "unless
  it kills you". e.g. {Repo Man}, {Vast Wealth}.[^1-1-2]
- **Mandatory holds for effects that look like a favour.** A cost reduction, a forced
  gain, a burn the text imposes on its own controller — none of these are the
  beneficiary's to decline, whoever ends up paying. e.g. {Serpent's Numbing Kiss},
  {Frondator}.[^1-1-3]
- **Mandatory holds when the effect is redundant.** An effect that will change nothing is
  still applied, and applying it still consumes any once-per-turn or once-per-game
  allowance attached to it. e.g. {Eze, The Demon Prince}, whose unlock triggers and is
  spent even on an action that already left him unlocked.[^1-1-4]

**Timing.** A mandatory effect is applied when its window arrives, and the acting player
must apply all of them before passing the opportunity to play effects to another player.
The window does not reopen: a controller who has declared his turn ended, or who has
passed on, does not get a forgotten mandatory effect applied retroactively.[^1-1-5]
Where the mandatory element is a *choice* between outcomes, there is no default. If every
player forgets it, the remedy is not to impose one branch after the fact.[^1-1-6]

**The optional side.** Text that *provides*, *permits* or *allows* creates an opportunity
and nothing more. Granting a minion a strike does not oblige him to strike with it;
granting a maneuver does not oblige him to maneuver; a master card that lets the vampire
it is on use an effect leaves the use entirely to that vampire's controller.[^1-1-7] The
same reading governs the reaction window — being awake, or having intercepted, obliges a
minion neither to attempt the block nor to keep playing reaction cards (§3.3 owns
this).[^1-1-8] Where the option sits inside an effect that resolves later, it is exercised
at resolution, not at announcement (§3.4).[^1-1-9]

Do not confuse a mandatory *effect*, which this section owns, with a mandatory *action* —
whether an obligation to act is discharged, and what a minion who cannot discharge it may
do instead, belongs to §3.9.

#### 1.1.2 What a printed "cannot" forbids

A "cannot" reaches exactly as far as its grammatical object, and the object is either the
play or the outcome. The two readings are not interchangeable.

- **"cannot play / cannot use / cannot be played" bars the play itself.** The card may not
  be announced at all, and no collateral benefit reopens it: not cycling it to draw a
  replacement, not putting it into the ash heap, not triggering some third effect that
  keys off having played a card. The archetype is a card that shuts a class of responses
  out of a combat — the opponent cannot play prevention cards even purely to cycle them,
  and cannot play an additional-strike card even when he wants it only for the side effect
  its other clause carries. e.g. {Blood Fury}, {Dead Hand}, {Rigor Mortis}.[^1-1-10]
- **"cannot be damaged / cannot gain blood / is immune to X" bars only that outcome.** It
  is not a prohibition on anyone's play. The card, ability or action that would have
  produced the barred outcome remains fully playable; it resolves, every other part of it
  applies normally, and only the forbidden component fails to happen. Where the rules
  provide a destination for the thwarted quantity, that destination is used rather than
  the effect failing: a vampire who cannot gain blood still hunts, and the blood he would
  have taken goes to the blood bank. e.g. {Charnas the Imp}, {Legacy of Caine}.[^1-1-11]

The practical rule for a judge is to read the verb the "cannot" governs before ruling on
the play. Nothing in this section licenses the wider claim that a card must accomplish
something to be playable. A prohibition is imposed by text; mere futility is not a
prohibition. Whether a card whose own text names an object may be played when no such
object exists — no equipment to steal, no damage to prevent, too few targets to allocate
its points to — is a question about that card's **requirements to play**, and §1.6 owns
the boundary (with §4.5 for the prevention-card case specifically). Do not generalise the
lockout rulings above into an answer to it: they concern a "cannot" one card imposes on
another player, not a card's own requirement.

#### 1.1.3 Reading card text

Card text is read against the game state at the moment it is applied, and every
application re-reads it.

- **Demonstratives are positional.** "this minion" is the minion playing or carrying the
  card; "that minion" is the one named by the immediately preceding clause, i.e. the
  target. The distinction is deliberate wherever both appear in one paragraph. e.g.
  {Big Game}.[^1-1-12]
- **Indexicals are re-evaluated, not fixed at announcement.** "you", "the acting
  Methuselah", "the minion being blocked" identify whoever satisfies the description when
  the text applies. An effect that changes who satisfies the description therefore
  disarms a second copy of itself: once the first {Major Boon} has been burned, "you" are
  no longer the Methuselah burning pool for that bleed, so a second cannot be burned
  against the same bleed.[^1-1-13] Whether copies otherwise stack is §1.15.
