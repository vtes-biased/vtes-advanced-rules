### 3.4 Resolution, success, and effect

**Base rules.** An action is successful when every block attempt has failed and no further
attempt is made; the cost is then paid and the effects of the successful action take place.
[RBK summary-of-the-course-of-an-action] Success is therefore settled by the block, before
resolution, and independently of what the action goes on to do.

#### 3.4.1 Success and effect are separate predicates

**An unblocked action is successful even when it accomplishes nothing.** Three wording
templates produce the same outcome and are ruled identically:

- the target's state has changed by the time the action resolves — "if the target unlocks
  and the action is not blocked, it is successful but has no effect", and the parallel
  wording for a target that no longer meets the action's requirements (e.g. {Ambush},
  {Harass});[^3-4-1]
- the acting minion no longer holds the card that granted the action — the provided action
  "ends with no effect ... it is still considered successful if not blocked" (e.g. {Bomb},
  {Chalice of Kinship});[^3-4-2]
- the target is removed or made illegal by another effect during the action (e.g.
  {Warsaw Station} bringing the Nosferatu out of torpor under a diablerie action,
  {Temptation} stealing the targeted minion).[^3-4-3]

The cost is still paid in all three cases: a null effect does not excuse payment, and the
acting minion stays locked.[^3-4-4] Nor does the loss of a legal target retroactively unwind
what already happened — a declared block attempt and any combat it produces still occur even
though the action can no longer have an effect (§3.3).[^3-4-5]

**Card text that says "successful" may mean the action, the outcome, or both — read which.**
The action succeeds when unblocked; whether the *thing the action was for* happened is a
second question. A hunt is the canonical case: the hunt action is successful when unblocked,
but "the hunt" is not successful unless blood was actually gained, so an effect gated on a
successful hunt does not fire (§3.7.2 owns this).[^3-4-6] Some cards compound the two
explicitly — {Abactor}'s "successful resolution" means the action was not blocked *and* the
vampire gained blood.[^3-4-7] The same split exists on the block side: where an effect makes
the action resolve as if unblocked, effects conditioned on a successful block do not see one
(§3.3).[^3-4-8]

#### 3.4.2 Everything the effect needs is read at resolution

Announcement fixes what the action *is* — target, cost, effects. Resolution fixes what it
*does*. Any state the effect depends on is read at resolution, not at announcement, and any
choice the effect leaves open is made then: which minion receives a counter, whether blood is
moved, which disciplines are copied, whether the card granting the effect is still in
play.[^3-4-9] A card set aside to perform the action is not "played" — and does not provide
its inherent effect — until the action reaches successful resolution; likewise a search
directed by an action card happens only if the action succeeds.[^3-4-10] Costs and abilities
that fund the action are likewise paid or used at resolution, and an additional cost is not
paid at all if the action does not succeed.[^3-4-11]

#### 3.4.3 Partial impossibility does not fail the action

An effect that cannot apply to some of its objects resolves for the rest: "the action will
simply have no effect on the things it cannot target" (e.g. {Edged Illusion}). The same holds
for an effect with several independent clauses — the clauses that can happen do, and the ones
that cannot are simply skipped (e.g. {Enticement}: the prey takes no damage if the Edge is
gone, but the bleed restriction still applies).[^3-4-12] Where the shortfall is an *amount*
rather than an object — a steal or transfer naming more than is there — see §1.7: the effect
takes what is available and the action is still successful.

#### 3.4.4 Effects conditioned on success versus on outcome

This is the practical payoff of §3.4.1, and the distinction is exhaustive:

- **"If the action is successful" reads success only.** Such a rider fires on an unblocked
  action that did nothing at all, and a card carrying it may be played knowing in advance that
  the action will have no effect — the unlock, the blood gain, and the extra action are all
  earned (e.g. {Forced March}, {Perfectionist}, {Freak Drive}).[^3-4-13]
- **"If X happens" reads the outcome.** A rider naming the thing the action was for — blood
  gained, the Edge burned, damage dealt — needs that thing to have happened.[^3-4-14]

Two corollaries. Futility does not release a requirement: a vampire under a compulsion to act
must take the action even when it is certain to accomplish nothing (§1.6 owns the
requirement-versus-futility boundary).[^3-4-15] And a no-effect action is not a cancelled one.
An announced action that resolves without effect leaves the minion locked; an action *card*
whose effect is nullified before it takes hold — cancelled, or reduced to "has no effect (no
cost is paid)" — never became a performed action, so the minion is not locked and may act
again (§1.8).[^3-4-16] Separately, effects that trigger on the action *reaching resolution*
are indifferent to both: they apply even if the action's own effect is null (§3.5).[^3-4-17]

#### 3.4.5 The "would be successful" window

Once every Methuselah has declined to block and passed, the action is about to resolve. A
distinct class of effect is playable in that final window — "only usable if a bleed would be
successful", "when the action would be successful" — after the point at which modifiers and
reactions are normally played.[^3-4-18]

**Playing one closes the ordinary window.** After such an effect is played, only other
"is/would be successful" effects may follow; anything that could redirect, cancel, or defeat
the action is now too late.[^3-4-19] Concretely: a card that must be played "before action
resolution" — a redirect, a target change, a bleed-cancelling reaction — cannot be played
after a "would be successful" effect has triggered, and neither can a reaction gated on the
action's success itself (e.g. {Archon Investigation} after {Spying Mission} burns for
+2 bleed).[^3-4-20] Effects that must be played before the bleed can be considered successful
are otherwise unconstrained by the block sequence: they may be played before or after block
attempts, so long as they precede the window (e.g. {Telepathic Counter}).[^3-4-21]

The window is defined by success, so an effect that destroys success closes it: a bleed
reduced to zero is no longer a successful bleed, and effects gated on a successful bleed
cannot be played afterwards (§3.7.1).[^3-4-22]

Effects worded "after action resolution" sit outside this window entirely and are not the same
as effects at resolution — where an action resolves before combat begins, "after resolution"
is deferred until the combat is over (§2.3).[^3-4-23]
